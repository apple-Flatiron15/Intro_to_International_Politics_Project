Popular Voting Systems

First-Past-the-Post (FPTP) Vote (aka 'Winner-Takes-All' system)

FPTP involves a single candidate within a electoral district winning the most amount of votes, even if it is not an absolute majority (at least 50% + 1) of all voting constituents. This is the simplest form of voting utilized in local, statewide, and national elections in various countries around the world, such as the United States (U.S.), Canada, and the United Kingdom (U.K.), although each of these countries have a number of jurisdictions that utilize other forms of voting in certain elections.

An alternative version of FPTP is 'At-Large Voting,' which involves multiple candidates running on one ballot in a single multi-member district. Voters are allowed to fill in as much or as little of the ballot as they want with the only limit being the total amount of seats that needs to be filled. Votes are then tallied for each candidate and the candidates with highest number of votes win those seats.

Alternative Vote (AV; aka 'Instant Runoff Vote (IRV)')

IRV involves voters ranking their most prefered candidates (with '1' being their first preference, '2' being their second, and so on) on a ballot in as many or as few marked boxes as they like. Afterwards, votes are tallied for everyone's first preferences and if no candidate has reach an absolute majority (see FPTP above) of votes, the candidate with the least amount of votes is eliminated and all of his or her votes are then transferred to their next [available] preference listed on the ballot. This process repeats until the candidate receives an absolute majority of votes and is declared the elected winner.

An alternative, but less-used, version of the IRV is the 'Two-Round Runoff System,' which involves a runoff election that involves exactly two candidates that received the most amount of votes, but not an absolute majority to win the first round. Hence, both candidates would seek support between the two rounds of election to win votes from their constituents.

Party-List Proportional Representation Vote (Open/Closed List)

Party-List Proportional Representation involves voters for their prefered political parties. Prior to the election, a select list of candidates from each party would be added to the ballot based on the number of [available] seats in each district. There are two main types of list systems: 'closed-list' and 'open-list':

  • In a 'closed party list,' the ballot would include the political parties and their select list of respective candidates would be arranged in order of preference of who they want in the legislature (usually the leader of the national or local party is listed first on the list of his or her district). Voters would only mark down the box of a political party that they prefered most and depending on the results, each political party would be assigned a seat equal to the proportion of 'popular votes' that it has received. Depending on their position on the list, each candidate on the ballot may or may not be assigned a seat based on the results mentioned in the previous statement.
  • For example, if a party wins approximately 60 percent of the vote in a 15-member district, then that party would be allowed to assign 9 seats, at most, to the top nine candidates on their list. The remaining 6 seats would be assigned to the other parties, if they've received enough votes to be assigned at least one seat on the ballot. If it's a national election for the [federal] legislature, the election results of each district would be added together for the final number of seats for each political party in the legislature.
  • In an 'open party list,' the ballot would include the political parties and checkboxes for each candidate below the names of their respective party. Voters would only mark down the box of a particular candidate, which would also count as a vote for that candidate's respective political party. If a political party wins at least 1 or 2 seats based on the proportion of 'popular votes' (see the 'closed party list' above), the seat(s) would then be allocated to the party's respective candidate(s) with the most amount of votes received.
